Help - press releases

In an ideal world, journalists writing about specialist research would have an understanding of the field, be able to read the original publication and distill the pertinent details out into a story which explains the significance of the research to the layperson.

So it should come as no surprise that most such articles are written almost entirely from a press release instead.

Such press releases are usually issued by the university or other institution which orginated the research, and a copy will be found on their web site.

Failing that, EurekAlert! publishes a large number of science press releases and has a search facility.

Help - Academic papers

Tracking down papers

Does the article mention publication of a paper in a particular journal? If so, try the journal's web site - most journal sites have a good search facility. Searching by author is a good technique as newspaper articles very often include quotes from researchers, mentioning them by name.

Does the article name the university or other institution where the research originated? It's likely the institution will have put out a press release which mentions a journal. Most institution web sites have a news section for press releases.

Researchers often put a full version of their paper up on their home page.

Keep in mind that very recent work might not yet even be online. Press releases are often sent out to the media before they are published on an institution's own web site. Research is often announced prior to appearing in a journal. In such cases, it's worth leaving things for a few days and coming back to it.

Academic search engines such as Google Scholar or Microsoft Academic Search can be helpful. It can take a day or two before new papers show up on such search engines, so they don't help for very recent articles.

Linking to papers

Academic journals often have confusing web sites, and a lot of them hide papers behind paywalls. So it's not always obvious where the best target for linking is.

Journal web sites have a summary page for each published paper. Even if the full paper is available, the summary page is a good place to link as it contains a lot of useful metadata that may not be present in the paper itself.

For journals with paywalls, the summary page is the only public page available.

Most importantly, summary pages usually contain the Digital Object Identifier (DOI) assigned to the paper. DOIs usually appear looking something like:
  doi:10.1080/000164800750000621
or, in URL form:
  http://dx.doi.org/10.1080/000164800750000621.

for example:

The DOI - if there is one - is the best way to uniquely identify a paper; DOIs are forever, while URLs can disappear and change over time (even though they really shouldn't).

Unsourced.org scans summary pages for DOIs. If it finds one, it can automatically fetch details about the paper such as title, publication date and the name of the journal. Alternatively, if you know the DOI already, you can enter it directly instead of the URL.

Can't find a DOI? No summary page? No problem, just use common sense and link somewhere useful. For example, you could link to a paper in PDF form on a researcher's personal web site.
